How the buying criteria differ for enterprise marketing teams

Enterprise marketing teams face a different set of operational realities than the marketers vendor sales decks are written for. The tool that wins for a Fortune-500 in-house team is rarely the right call for a 4-person agency or a Shopify store. What follows is the buyer’s checklist I use when sizing up a tool for the enterprise marketing teams pattern specifically.

The five criteria that matter

  • Spend-tier fit. Tools have implicit minimum-spend assumptions. If your accounts are below ~$30K/mo, ML-based tools can’t learn fast enough to deliver lift in a useful window. Enterprise marketing teams need to filter for tools that work at their actual spend tier, not the demo’s.
  • Onboarding hours. A tool that takes 40 operator hours to deploy is structurally different from one that takes 4. For enterprise marketing teams, the hours are the cost.
  • Repeatability across accounts. If implementation requires deep operator expertise, it can’t scale to a 10+ account book. Repeatability is the gate criterion that eliminates most of the “genuinely great but” tools.
  • Pricing that doesn’t penalize growth. Some tools tier by account count, some by spend, some by feature. The right model for enterprise marketing teams avoids the cliff where adding one client doubles the bill.
  • Vendor responsiveness. When something breaks, can you reach an actual engineer in under a day? Enterprise vendors often have multi-day SLAs that don’t work when your client’s campaigns are live.

What I’d avoid for enterprise marketing teams

  • Enterprise multi-channel platforms with annual minimums. They sound impressive on a demo but their unit economics break for enterprise marketing teams-shaped accounts.
  • Rule-only tools dressed as AI. If the “AI” turns out to be a configurable rules engine, you’re writing the strategy by hand. That’s fine if you have the time, but it’s not what enterprise marketing teams are buying when they buy an AI tool.
  • Tools that require a 6-month rollout. If you can’t see lift in a 90-day pilot, the tool’s not fit for the speed at which enterprise marketing teams need to make tooling decisions.
